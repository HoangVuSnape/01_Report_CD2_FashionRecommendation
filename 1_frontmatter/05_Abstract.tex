\chapter*{INTEGRATING MULTIMODAL REPRESENTATIONS INTO GRAPH-BASED FASHION RECOMMENDER SYSTEMS\\ABSTRACT}

Personalized fashion recommender systems face severe challenges due to high user-item interaction sparsity and the limitations of ID-bound collaborative filtering models. To address these bottlenecks, this study investigates multimodal graph collaborative filtering by integrating visual and textual representations of clothing items into Graph Neural Network (GNN) architectures. We systematically benchmark four models---LightGCN, CombiGCN (adapted to operate over item similarity graphs), BM3, and FREEDOM---under twenty-four model configurations. These configurations utilize diverse feature encoders (MobileNetV2, CLIP, BERT, and TF-IDF) and fusion paradigms (late fusion average pooling vs. weighted attention gating) on the Vibrent Clothes Rental (VCR) dataset. Empirical results reveal three key findings. First, the MobileNetV2 vision architecture consistently outperforms the CLIP vision-language foundation model, demonstrating that localized spatial texture descriptors are more highly effective for garment styling compatibility than broad cross-modal semantic representations. Second, the parameter-free late fusion strategy serves as a highly stable and robust baseline; in contrast, introducing trainable parameters via weighted attention gating induces semantic noise and overfitting when applied to sparse interaction logs. Third, model superiority is retrieval-depth dependent: BM3, utilizing self-supervised contrastive bootstrap alignment, achieves state-of-the-art performance for ranking lists of size $K \ge 5$, whereas the adapted CombiGCN dual-graph model exhibits superior accuracy for strict top-1 single-item recommendations. Overall, this study demonstrates that embedding multimodal features into GNNs effectively alleviates data sparsity, providing critical empirical guidelines for designing personalized fashion recommendation systems.
